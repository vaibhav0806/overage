# Overage — Maker Story

## The story

Last year, I sat down on a slow Sunday and did something I'd been avoiding: I went through every project I'd worked on in the past 18 months and wrote down every thing I built that wasn't in the original scope.

The extra admin dashboard. The "quick" PDF export. The two rounds of revisions that became five. The login flow that wasn't in the brief but "obviously" needed to be there.

I put dollar values next to each one. The total was $11,000.

Eleven thousand dollars of work I did and never billed for. Not because I didn't notice — I noticed every single time. But each addition felt too small to make a thing about. A $300 contact form here, a $500 set of changes there. I'd think, "it's not worth the awkward conversation," and just do it.

I looked for a tool that could help. I tried everything. Toggl tracks time. Harvest tracks time. Asana tracks tasks. Linear tracks tasks. None of them answered the one question that actually mattered: **how much extra work did I do beyond what we agreed, and what is that worth in dollars?**

That gap — between the original scope and what I actually delivered — that's where all the money was leaking. And nothing tracked it.

So I built Overage.

The idea is dead simple. When a client asks for something that wasn't in the scope, you log it. Takes about 10 seconds — give it a name, assign a dollar value, done. Overage keeps a running total for every project. You can see, at any point, exactly how much unpaid scope creep has accumulated.

But the part that actually changed things for me was the report. When the additions stack up, you hit a button and Overage generates a clean, professional scope change document — line items, dollar amounts, dates, the works. Instead of drafting an uncomfortable email at 11 PM trying to figure out how to say "you owe me more money," you send a document. It reframes the whole conversation. You're not complaining. You're being professional.

The first time I sent one to a client, I was nervous. They responded in 20 minutes: "Makes sense, let's add this to the next invoice." That was $3,200 I would have eaten in silence.

Here's what I believe: freelancers don't have a confidence problem. They have a tooling problem. Give someone a professional way to document scope changes and they'll use it. The awkward conversation becomes a business conversation.

Overage is free for one project. If it works for you, it's $8/month for unlimited projects. One recovered scope change pays for a year.

I built this because I was tired of doing free work. I think a lot of you are too.
