## Problem statement

Freelancers routinely absorb scope additions because each one feels too small to push back on. A contact form here, a revision round there — individually minor, collectively devastating. Over a year, this silent bleed costs the average freelancer thousands of dollars. And the tools that exist — time trackers, project managers, spreadsheets — none of them frame deviations against the original agreement as money owed.

## Solution statement

Overage lets you log scope additions in 10 seconds, attach a dollar value, and see exactly how much unpaid work has accumulated on every project. When it's time to address it with your client, you generate a professional scope change report — a document that reframes an awkward conversation into a business discussion backed by clear data.

## Objection responses

### "I can do this in a spreadsheet"

You can. Most freelancers try. The problem is that a spreadsheet isn't something you'd send to a client. It looks informal, it requires manual formatting, and it sits in a tab you never open. Overage gives you a system that's fast enough to actually use and produces a document professional enough to actually send.

### "I don't want to seem difficult with my clients"

That's exactly why we built the report. Sending a spreadsheet or bringing it up over coffee feels confrontational. A professional scope change document reframes it — you're not complaining, you're documenting. Clients respect it because it looks like something a serious business sends, not a grievance. Most users report that clients respond better than expected.

### "My projects are too small for this"

Small projects are where scope creep hurts the most. On a $2,000 project, a few "small" additions worth $600 means you just lost 30% of your margin. And because it's a small project, you're least likely to push back. Overage is built for exactly this scenario.

### "$8/mo is too expensive"

If Overage helps you recover even one $200 scope addition per year, it's paid for itself 2x over. Most users track that much in their first week. The free tier lets you try it on one project with zero risk.

### "I already use Toggl / Harvest / Asana"

Those are great tools — for what they do. Toggl and Harvest track time. Asana tracks tasks. None of them answer the question: "How much extra work did I do beyond what was agreed, and what is it worth?" Overage fills that specific gap. It's not a replacement; it's the piece that's missing.

## Voice and tone guidelines

### We are
- **Direct** — Say what we mean. No fluff, no filler, no corporate hedging.
- **Warm** — We're on the freelancer's side. We've been there. The tone should feel like advice from a friend who's also a freelancer, not a SaaS company.
- **Professional** — Our product helps people have professional conversations. Our own communication should reflect that.
- **Slightly bold** — We're willing to say things like "stop doing free work." Not aggressive, but not timid.

### We are NOT
- **Corporate** — No "leverage synergies" or "streamline workflows." We talk like people.
- **Salesy** — No fake urgency, no "limited time offers," no manipulation. The problem sells itself.
- **Cutesy** — No excessive exclamation marks, no quirky mascots, no trying too hard to be fun. The topic is serious (people's income) and we respect that.
- **Technical** — Our users are often technical, but our marketing shouldn't be. No jargon, no feature specs in headlines.

### Writing rules
- Use "you" more than "we." It's about the freelancer, not us.
- Specific numbers beat vague claims. "$3,200 in tracked additions" > "significant savings."
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Respect people's time.
- When in doubt, ask: "Would a freelancer friend say this over coffee?" If not, rewrite it.
